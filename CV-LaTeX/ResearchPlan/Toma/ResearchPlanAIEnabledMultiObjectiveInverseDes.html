AI-Enabled Multi-Objective Inverse Design of Chiral Metasurfaces for Biosensing via Moth-Eye Template Nanofabrication

1. Abstract

This research plan outlines a project to develop a novel Artificial Intelligence (AI) framework for the inverse design of chiral plasmonic metasurfaces, specifically tailored for the "Moth-eye film" template fabrication process recently employed in the Toma laboratory. This work introduces three key innovations to address critical gaps in the current state-of-the-art: (1) a generative, on-demand design methodology using a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), moving beyond slower iterative search algorithms; (2) a fabrication-aware approach where the AI is "taught" the specific geometric constraints of the Toma lab's "Moth-eye film" template process, ensuring all generated designs are inherently manufacturable; and (3) a multi-objective optimization strategy to synergistically co-optimize for both chiroptical response (Circular Dichroism) and colorimetric sensing sensitivity. By encoding manufacturing rules directly into a parameter-based conditional GAN (cGAN), this project will bridge the persistent "design-to-fabrication gap". The project will culminate in my hands-on fabrication and experimental validation of the AI-generated designs, demonstrating a complete design-to-device pipeline. The ultimate goal is to deliver a library of high-performance, experimentally viable designs for the next generation of spectrometer-free chiral biosensors, directly empowering the experimental capabilities of the Toma group.

2. Research Background and Innovation

2.1. State-of-the-Art in the Toma Laboratory

Professor Mana Toma's research group is at the forefront of translating plasmonic phenomena into practical applications, having strategically pivoted towards spectrometer-free, colorimetric biosensors designed for mobile health diagnostics.1 A significant recent advancement is the use of industrially manufactured "moth-eye films" as templates for the low-cost, scalable fabrication of metal nanopillar arrays. This technique establishes a robust fabrication pathway but also imposes specific geometric constraints. Concurrently, the lab has begun exploring AI for metamaterial design, signaling a clear interest in computational design paradigms.2

2.2. Gaps in Current Research and Proposed Innovations

While the field of AI-driven metasurface design is active, recent publications reveal critical gaps that this project aims to fill. For instance, some state-of-the-art methods employ iterative search algorithms like Evolution Strategies (ES) for design optimization. While powerful, these methods must re-run the entire optimization process for each new target. Other approaches use generative models but often produce freeform, pixel-based designs that are incompatible with scalable fabrication techniques and focus on optimizing only a single performance metric.

This research plan introduces a multi-layered innovation to overcome these limitations:

  • Methodological Innovation: A Generative, On-Demand Framework. Unlike iterative search algorithms, this project will employ a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN). Once trained, a GAN learns the entire design landscape and can generate a diverse library of optimized designs for new targets almost instantly. This transitions the design process from a slow, one-off search to a rapid, on-demand generative tool, perfectly suited for exploring complex design trade-offs.
  • Strategic Innovation: Hyper-Specialization with Fabrication-Aware Design. This is the core novelty of the project. Instead of generating unconstrained pixel images, the AI will be trained to output a vector of physical parameters (e.g., pillar height, diameter, period, rotation) that are directly mapped to the geometric possibilities of the Toma lab's moth-eye template process. This "parameterization" approach hard-codes manufacturing constraints into the AI model, ensuring every design is, by definition, fabricable. This directly bridges the critical "design-fabrication gap" that limits the practical utility of many AI design studies.
  • Application-Specific Innovation: Multi-Objective Co-Optimization. This project moves beyond simple spectrum matching. The AI will be explicitly trained to navigate the complex trade-offs required for a superior biosensor by simultaneously co-optimizing for two distinct physical objectives: maximizing the intrinsic chiroptical signal (Circular Dichroism) for specificity, and maximizing the colorimetric shift upon molecular binding (Sensing Sensitivity) for detectability. This aligns with the growing need for multi-objective approaches in nanophotonics and the specific application of chiral biosensing.

3. Problem Statement

While AI-driven inverse design is promising, current frameworks are ill-suited for the specific needs of an experimental group like Professor Toma's.5 They often produce designs incompatible with scalable fabrication methods like the moth-eye template process and fail to co-optimize for the multiple, competing performance metrics essential for high-performance biosensors. The design of chiral metasurfaces, in particular, is crucial for enhancing the weak signals from biomolecules but presents a complex optimization challenge. This creates a critical need for a specialized AI-driven design framework that can:

  1. Operate exclusively within the geometric design space defined by the moth-eye template process, guaranteeing manufacturability.
  2. Simultaneously optimize for both strong chiroptical signal and high sensing sensitivity, a complex requirement not addressed by single-objective methods.
  3. Rapidly generate a diverse portfolio of viable design candidates, enabling an informed selection process that balances peak performance with fabrication tolerance.

4. Proposed Work and Objectives

The proposed work is to develop, train, and validate a novel AI-driven inverse design framework based on a conditional Generative Adversarial Network (cGAN) that is specifically tailored to the moth-eye fabrication process.7

Primary Objective: To create a multi-objective, fabrication-aware AI tool that can automatically generate geometric parameters for chiral metasurfaces that exhibit both strong circular dichroism and high colorimetric sensing sensitivity.

Key Sub-Objectives:

  • Objective 1: Digitize the Moth-Eye Design Space. To define a parametric model that mathematically describes the range of achievable nanostructure geometries using the moth-eye template method.
  • Objective 2: Develop a Novel Generative AI Model with Encoded Manufacturing Constraints. To build and train a cGAN where the input "condition" is a vector of desired performance targets and the output is a set of manufacturable geometric parameters. This leverages my experience building optical neural networks with PyTorch.8
  • Objective 3: Generate a Library of Optimized, Manufacturable Designs. To utilize the trained AI to generate a comprehensive library of novel chiral metasurface designs.
  • Objective 4: Experimentally Fabricate and Validate the AI-Generated Designs. To personally fabricate the top candidate designs, characterize their morphology and optical properties, and perform sensing experiments to validate the entire "design-to-device" pipeline.

5. Methods

The methodology is a multi-stage process that directly leverages my background in computational physics, AI, and experimental characterization.

5.1 Data Generation via Automated FDTD Simulation: The foundation of the AI model will be a high-quality dataset. I will use my proficiency in FDTD simulations to develop an automated Python script that samples thousands of unique geometric parameter sets from the defined "Moth-Eye Design Space" and simulates their optical and sensing responses to build the foundational dataset.8

5.2 Development of a Parameter-Based cGAN: I will implement the core AI framework using the PyTorch library.8

  • Manufacturability by Design: The Generator network will be designed to output a vector of geometric parameters (e.g., [h, d, p, Δx, θ]) rather than a pixelated image. This parameterization method is a key innovation, distinguishing this work from common pixel-based models by ensuring every AI-generated design is inherently compatible with the moth-eye template process.
  • Multi-Objective Conditioning: The cGAN will be conditioned on a target performance vector, allowing us to request designs that meet complex, multi-faceted criteria. This directly addresses the multi-objective nature of the problem, leveraging my experience with optimization algorithms.
  • Surrogate Model for Efficiency: A forward-predicting neural network (a "surrogate model") will be trained first on the FDTD dataset. This surrogate can predict the performance of a given structure in milliseconds and will be used within the GAN's loss function to rapidly evaluate generated designs during training.

5.3 Inverse Design and Library Generation: Once trained, the cGAN will be used as a rapid design tool to generate a diverse library of candidate designs. This solves the "one-to-many" mapping problem, providing multiple structural solutions for a single desired optical response, which is crucial for selecting a design that is robust to fabrication tolerances.6

5.4 Experimental Fabrication and Characterization: This crucial phase will be led by myself to close the design-fabrication-test loop.

  • Fabrication: Following the protocol established by the Toma lab, I will fabricate the top-ranked AI-generated design. This process involves using the commercial moth-eye film as a nanoimprint template and depositing a metal (e.g., Silver or Gold) of a precise thickness via thermal evaporation to form the final nanopillar array.
  • Morphological Characterization: I will personally utilize my hands-on experience with Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) to image the fabricated metasurfaces.8 The resulting high-resolution images will be analyzed to measure the actual geometric parameters and assess the fabrication fidelity by comparing them directly against the AI-generated design blueprint.
  • Optical Validation: I will perform optical characterization of the fabricated chips to calculate the experimental Circular Dichroism (CD) spectrum ($CD = R_{LCP} - R_{RCP}$) and compare it with the FDTD simulation predictions.10
  • Biosensing Test: To validate the sensor's performance, I will conduct a proof-of-concept biosensing experiment. This will involve functionalizing the chip surface and introducing a model chiral biomolecule to quantify its experimental sensitivity, thus validating the multi-objective optimization goal.7

6. Evaluation and Success Criteria

  • Dataset Quality: Generation of a dataset with over 10,000 unique, converged simulations covering the defined parameter space.
  • AI Model Performance: The trained cGAN must generate design parameters that, when validated by FDTD simulation, meet the input target performance vector with less than a 10% deviation.
  • Fabrication and Characterization: Successful fabrication of at least one AI-designed metasurface, with geometric features (verified by my own SEM analysis) matching the design parameters within a 15% tolerance.8
  • Experimental Performance Validation (Ultimate Goal): The fabricated device must exhibit a measurable chiroptical response and demonstrate a detectable colorimetric/spectral shift upon exposure to a model analyte, with the results qualitatively matching the trends predicted by the simulations.

7. Timeline and Milestones (24 Months)

  • Months 1-6: Foundation and Data Generation
  • Tasks: In-depth literature review. Consult with Prof. Toma to finalize the parametric design space. Develop automated FDTD simulation pipeline. Generate the core training dataset.8
  • Milestone 1: A complete, curated dataset of at least 10,000 "parameter-performance" pairs.
  • Months 7-12: AI Model Development and Training
  • Tasks: Implement the surrogate model and the parameter-based cGAN architecture in PyTorch. Train and optimize the full cGAN framework.8
  • Milestone 2: A trained AI framework capable of generating manufacturable design parameters based on multi-objective performance inputs.
  • Months 13-18: Inverse Design, Optimization, and Analysis
  • Tasks: Use the trained AI to generate a library of high-performance designs. Select top candidates for fabrication.
  • Milestone 3: A comprehensive "Design Library" of optimized, manufacturable chiral metasurfaces.
  • Months 19-24: Experimental Validation and Thesis Completion
  • Tasks: I will personally lead the fabrication, SEM characterization, optical measurement, and initial biosensing tests of the top-ranked AI design.8 Analyze experimental data and complete the master's thesis.
  • Milestone 4: Successful Master's thesis defense, demonstrating a closed loop from AI-driven design to my own experimental validation. Submission of a manuscript to a high-impact journal.
